# Information Organization Skills for Media Literacy

Media literacy depends on organizing claims, sources, evidence, context, questions, screenshots, and revisions. Information Organization helps kids keep claims connected to where they came from.

This page is the local doorway into the Literacy for Kids [Information Organization Toolkit](https://www.literacy-for-kids.com/docs/toolkits/information-organization), connected to the media analysis this curriculum builds.

## A few core ideas

- **Claims, sources, and evidence should stay connected.** A claim that lost its source is hard to check.
- **Screenshots without source and context can become confusing** — or misleading.
- **Source trackers help later checking.** Breadcrumbs back to where it came from.
- **Categories help compare media messages.** Fact, opinion, claim, and evidence are different piles.
- **Notes should preserve questions, not just answers.**

## When this shows up

- When checking a claim
- When comparing sources
- When tracking evidence
- When collecting screenshots
- When planning a media message
- When revising a project after feedback
- When sorting fact, opinion, claim, and evidence

## Tools that help

- **Claim / source / evidence tracker** — keep the trio together.
- **Fact vs. opinion vs. claim sort** — three piles before judging.
- **Screenshot context note** — where, when, and what it shows.
- **Source category table** — what kind of source is this?
- **Revision notes** — what feedback said and what changed.

:::tip Information Organization Moment
A claim should stay connected to its source. When you write down a claim, also write where it came from and what evidence supports it. That keeps the information checkable later.
:::

:::caution Everyday organization skills, not a replacement for support
These are everyday information organization tools, not therapy, disability evaluation, academic intervention, legal advice, medical advice, or a replacement for trusted adults, educators, caregivers, accommodations, or qualified support. Kids should not be treated as lazy or broken when information feels messy or hard to organize. If information feels consistently overwhelming, inaccessible, unsafe, private, sensitive, or too big, involve a trusted adult and appropriate support.
:::
